Your First PT Visit at Chardon Performance Therapy

By Dave Nelson, PT, DPT, CIDN · April 2, 2026

If you've never been to physical therapy — or you've been to the kind where you're handed a sheet of exercises and told to find an empty mat — you probably don't know what to expect. That's fair, and we don't assume otherwise.

Here's what your first visit at Chardon Performance Therapy actually looks like, minute by minute.

Before you walk in

When you book, we email you a short digital intake packet — typically less than ten minutes to fill out. It asks the basics: what hurts, how long, what you've tried, any relevant medical history, goals.

We're a cash-based, out-of-network practice — no insurance billing surprises. You'll know the flat per-visit price up front, and we provide a superbill for potential out-of-network reimbursement. HSA/FSA accepted.

A day before, you get a text reminder. That's the extent of what you have to do.

When you arrive

You walk into our main clinic at 520 Fifth Avenue, Suite #3. We don't have a big waiting room with a TV because we don't run a big patient-stacking clinic. You check in, grab water if you want, and within a couple minutes your therapist comes to meet you.

From that moment until the end of the appointment, you're with your assigned licensed physical therapist — one of our clinicians (Dave, Courtney, Megan, or Brad). No aides. No techs. No juggling three patients on three tables.

Minutes 0–15: Conversation

This part surprises new patients more than anything else. We spend real time — 15 minutes — just talking.

We ask you to walk us through what's going on in your own words. When did it start, what triggered it (or didn't), what does it feel like, where exactly, what makes it better, what makes it worse, how has it changed, what do you do for work, what do you do for fun, and what are your actual goals — not just "less pain" but the thing you can't do right now that you want back.

The reason we spend this long is simple: by the end of this conversation, a good clinician has a working list of the 3–5 most likely drivers. The physical exam is then designed to confirm or rule them out — not to fish in the dark.

Minutes 15–35: Hands-on assessment

Now we move. We watch how you walk. We have you squat, hinge, reach overhead — whatever is relevant. We test specific ranges of motion with a goniometer. We test strength with a handheld dynamometer. We run orthopedic tests specific to the structures we suspect.

If you've had imaging (MRI, X-ray), we look at it with you. But here's something most people don't realize: imaging findings don't always explain pain. Lots of 40-year-olds have "degenerative disc disease" on MRI and zero symptoms. Lots of 25-year-olds have clean MRIs and real pain. Our job is to correlate what the image shows with what your body is actually doing.

We also look at related joints, not just the painful one. A stiff ankle drives a lot of knee pain. A weak hip drives a lot of low back pain. A stiff mid-back drives a lot of shoulder pain. The source is rarely where the symptom lives.

Minutes 35–50: Explanation and first treatment

We sit down and walk through findings in plain English. No jargon. We draw things if it helps. You get to ask every question on your mind — and more — until you understand what we found and what the plan is.

Then we start treatment. First sessions typically include some combination of:

  • Manual therapy for the tissues that came up stiff or irritable
  • Dry needling if it's indicated and you're comfortable with it
  • Movement correction — a few specific drills for what was off
  • Pain-science education if symptoms have been chronic

The goal of the first-session treatment isn't to "fix" you in 15 minutes. It's to change something measurable — a range of motion, a provocative test, a functional movement — so you leave different than you came in, and so we both have a data point to track progress against.

Minutes 50–60: The plan

You leave with a written plan. It includes:

  • What we found, stated simply
  • A realistic timeline (how many visits, over how many weeks)
  • Your home program — usually 3–4 specific exercises with sets/reps, written out and sent to your phone with video demos
  • What we'll do at the next visit and when we'll re-check progress
  • Red flags — what would make us want to hear from you sooner

You check out, book your follow-up, and go. The whole thing takes about 65–70 minutes door-to-door.

Between visits

We give you our email. If a question comes up, or something changes — you text or email. We actually respond. The reason we can do that is because we don't have a caseload of 40 patients a day. Less volume, more attention. It's the model.

Visit 2, 3, and beyond

Follow-ups are also a full hour with the same therapist. We check how the home program is going, recheck the measures that were off at baseline, progress what's progressing, adjust what isn't, and do treatment for the session. We write new exercises into the program as you earn them. We graduate exercises as they stop being hard.

By visit 3–4 we should be seeing measurable improvement. If we aren't, we don't just do the same thing again — we reconsider the diagnosis, talk about imaging or a specialist referral if needed, and decide together on the next step.

How long will PT take?

Most plans are 6–12 visits over 4–8 weeks. Simple cases resolve faster. Chronic or post-surgical cases take longer. We'll give you an estimate at visit one and re-estimate every few visits.

The goal is to get you out of PT. Not to keep you on a schedule. At discharge, you'll have a home program you own, education on what to watch for, and an open line back if something flares. That's a win.

A few things patients often ask

"Do I need a referral?" No — Ohio is a direct-access state. Some insurance plans require one; we'll tell you before your first visit.

"What should I wear?" Comfortable, loose clothing you can move in. Shorts and a t-shirt are ideal — especially if the issue is hip, knee, or ankle.

"Should I bring my imaging?" Yes, if you have it. Even a phone photo is fine.

"Will it hurt?" PT shouldn't cause sharp pain. Mild soreness during/after manual work or needling is normal. We calibrate intensity to you, not to a protocol.

"What if PT doesn't work?" We'll tell you. If we aren't making progress, we'll change the plan, consult, or refer — never just keep running the clock.

If you're thinking about booking

The best time to come in is earlier than you think. Most patients come in after 3–6 months of self-managing. Almost all of them say, "I should have come sooner."

If you want to see if PT is right for you before committing to a full evaluation, we offer a free 15-minute phone consult. We'll listen, ask a few questions, and tell you honestly whether we're the right fit — or refer you somewhere better if we aren't.
