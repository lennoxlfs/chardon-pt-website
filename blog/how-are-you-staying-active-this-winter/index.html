How Are You Staying Active This Winter?

By Chardon PT Team · February 4, 2026

Winter in Northeast Ohio doesn't have to mean slowing down. At Chardon Performance Therapy, we work with driven, active people who don't sit still just because the temperature drops. Our running expert Courtney is a great example — she keeps training outdoors through the winter. And when she's not logging miles, she's out cross-country skiing with her son Miles.

Staying active in the cold isn't about being extreme. It's about staying consistent.

Running in the snow — smart and safe

If you're heading out for a winter run, here's how to stay strong and safe:

1. Layer strategically

Three layers is the move — a moisture-wicking base, an insulating mid, and a wind-resistant outer shell. Avoid cotton. It traps sweat and chills you fast once you stop moving.

2. Protect your extremities

Gloves, thermal socks, and a hat or headband go a long way. You lose significant heat through your head and hands — more than most people realize.

3. Improve traction

Consider trail shoes or traction cleats when snow or ice is on the ground. Shorten your stride and increase cadence to reduce slipping. Big, heel-first strides on ice are a recipe for a hip-slam fall.

4. Warm up inside first

Five to ten minutes of dynamic mobility indoors reduces strain when you hit cold air. Hips, calves, thoracic spine — wake them up before you ask them to perform.

5. Be visible

Winter means darker conditions. Wear reflective gear and consider a headlamp for early morning or evening runs. Drivers in Northeast Ohio are not looking for runners in February.

6. Adjust expectations

Snowy miles are strength miles. Your pace will drop — but muscular demand goes up. Embrace it. Winter training builds a base that shows up in your summer splits.

Cross-country skiing — a full-body winter win

Courtney also took advantage of fresh snow by heading out cross-country skiing with Miles. Why we love it:

  • Low-impact cardio — easy on the joints after a hard run week
  • Full-body engagement — arms, core, hips, and legs all working
  • Excellent aerobic base training
  • Great balance and coordination challenge
  • Built-in family time

It's proof that winter movement doesn't have to mean grinding through workouts in a basement gym. It can mean building endurance and making memories in the same afternoon.

Why staying active in the cold matters

When temperatures drop, it's easy to slip into a cycle of reduced activity → stiffness → aches → lost momentum. Consistent winter movement helps:

  • Maintain cardiovascular fitness
  • Support immune function
  • Improve mood and reduce seasonal blues
  • Preserve strength and joint mobility
  • Build resilience heading into spring training

Cold weather training also develops mental toughness. You prove to yourself that conditions don't dictate commitment — and that carries into every other part of your life.

Don't let winter steal your momentum

If you've been feeling:

  • "My body feels tighter this time of year"
  • "I don't want to lose all my progress"
  • "I'm not sure how to train safely in the cold"

You're not alone. The key is having a plan that aligns with your goals and the current season. Whether that's winter running guidance, gait analysis, strength programming, or just accountability — we're here to help you stay in control of your body year-round.

Winter isn't the off-season for your health. It's the season where consistency compounds.
