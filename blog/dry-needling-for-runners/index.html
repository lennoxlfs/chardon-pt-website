Dry Needling for Runners: What It Does, What It Doesn't

By Courtney Crawford, PT, CIDN · April 9, 2026

If you're a runner, you've either had someone suggest dry needling or you've seen it on Instagram. Either way, you probably have questions. As a CIDN-certified PT who uses needles on runners every day, here's the honest version — what the research supports, what my patients actually experience, and when I tell runners to skip it.

First — what dry needling actually is

Dry needling is a technique where a licensed physical therapist inserts a very thin, sterile monofilament needle into a specific muscle or tendon. It's not acupuncture — different philosophy, different targets, different framework. We're targeting the tissue directly: usually a myofascial trigger point, a band of hypertonic muscle, or a tendon attachment that isn't responding to other care.

What happens when the needle reaches a trigger point is often a small, brief muscle twitch — a "local twitch response." Patients describe it as a deep cramp that fades in a second or two. That twitch is the mechanism: a brief reset of the over-firing muscle fibers. Research shows it's accompanied by measurable changes in muscle activity, local circulation, and pain sensitivity.

It's not magic. It's also not placebo — the evidence for certain conditions is strong enough to put dry needling in most modern PT clinical guidelines.

The four most-asked spots for runners

1. Glute medius / piriformis / deep hip rotators (for hip and low back pain)

This is the one I use needling for most in runners, hands down. Chronic deep-glute tightness is the silent driver behind a surprising number of running injuries: piriformis syndrome, SI joint irritation, chronic low back pain, even some "hip labrum" symptoms.

The deep glute/hip rotator complex is famously hard to stretch or foam-roll effectively because it sits underneath a lot of tissue. A 3–4 minute needling session can do what an hour of rolling cannot. My patients routinely stand up after treatment and say their hip feels 20% looser on the first step. That's not an exaggeration — it's the norm.

What it does: meaningful reduction in deep muscular restriction that's driving pain. What it doesn't: replace the glute strengthening that prevents it from coming back. Needling buys you a window. Rehab keeps it open.

2. Calf complex (gastroc, soleus) for runner's calf and Achilles issues

Soleus — the deep calf muscle — is the runner's engine. It's also one of the most commonly overloaded tissues in distance runners, especially anyone coming back from a layoff or ramping mileage. Chronic soleus tightness is a huge contributor to Achilles tendon problems and medial calf strains.

Needling the soleus and gastroc (the big two calf muscles) is efficient. The tissue response is fast. Most of my runners report noticeable improvement in calf stiffness after one session and much better function during push-off.

Caveat: for an actual Achilles tendinopathy (tendon, not muscle), the evidence for needling the tendon itself is mixed. What helps much more reliably: needling the calf muscles upstream to remove tension from the tendon, combined with progressive heavy calf strengthening (the gold standard). Isolated tendon needling alone, without rehab, rarely moves the needle.

3. IT band / tensor fasciae latae / vastus lateralis (for lateral knee pain)

"IT band syndrome" is one of the most misunderstood running diagnoses. The IT band itself doesn't really stretch — it's a thick fibrous tissue. What drives ITBS is usually the muscles that feed into it (TFL and vastus lateralis, the outer quad) being chronically tight and overactive while the glute medius underperforms.

Needling the TFL and vastus lateralis can meaningfully reduce the pulling force on the IT band. Pairing that with targeted glute medius strengthening and a running-form check is the program I use with most ITBS runners, and it consistently shortens recovery compared to stretching/foam-rolling alone.

What it does: reduces upstream muscle tension causing the IT band irritation. What it doesn't: fix a muscle weakness or a stride pattern that's perpetually loading it the wrong way.

4. Plantar fascia & intrinsic foot muscles (for plantar fasciitis)

Plantar fasciitis is a classic case where needling can be very useful — but only as one piece of a larger plan. The evidence is decent for needling the calf complex, the posterior tibialis, and the intrinsic foot muscles (the small muscles inside the arch). These often co-drive plantar fascia overload.

Combined with calf mobility, short foot strengthening, and load management, dry needling in plantar fasciitis often shortens the timeline from months to weeks. By itself — just needling, no rehab — results are unreliable. Again: needling is a tool, not a treatment plan.

What dry needling is not for

  • Acute muscle tears. If you felt a "pop" and it turned purple, keep the needle away. Let tissue heal first.
  • Bone pain. Stress fractures and bone stress reactions need imaging and rest, not needles.
  • Nerve pain alone. If the symptom is electrical, burning, or numb-and-tingling, the priority is finding the nerve source. Needling can sometimes help with a downstream muscular component, but it's not the primary lever.
  • A substitute for strength training. I say this every day: needling buys the window, training keeps it open. Runners who skip the rehab come back in 6 weeks with the same problem.

What the first session feels like

New patients are usually a little apprehensive — reasonably. Here's the real experience:

  • Needles are the thickness of a hair. You rarely feel them go in.
  • When the needle reaches the target, you'll feel a deep ache or a quick twitch. Sharp pain should not happen.
  • Sessions are short — usually 5–15 minutes on a specific area, often paired with other treatment.
  • Post-treatment soreness is common for 24 hours, similar to a hard workout. Heat, light movement, hydration.
  • You can usually run within 24–48 hours — we'll talk through your specific case.

How I decide whether to needle

Needling isn't the first tool. It's one of several. When I'm evaluating a runner, the decision goes something like this:

  • Is there a clear muscular component (trigger points, hypertonic tissue, neural inhibition)? — if yes, needling is on the table.
  • Is the patient comfortable with it? — informed consent every time.
  • Is there a contraindication? (blood thinners with some caveats, active infection at the site, needle phobia, etc.) — if yes, we use alternative manual techniques.
  • Is the bigger issue a strength/mobility/stride pattern that needling won't solve? — if so, needling is adjunctive, not primary.

The short answer runners want

Yes, dry needling can be a great tool for runners dealing with persistent hip, calf, IT band, or foot pain — especially when those issues haven't responded to a few weeks of rest, stretching, and rolling.

No, it's not magic, it's not a replacement for strength training, and it's not always indicated. A good clinician tells you which category you're in before they reach for a needle.
